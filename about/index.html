About droptimize.org

Built by fixing
our own mistakes.

droptimize.org didn't start as a service. It started as a process we built for ourselves - auditing every OYE Creations property, fixing what was broken, and writing down every rule we learned. This is that story, and what it means for your site.

The origin

We audited ourselves first.

Every OYE Creations property - the podcast site, the resume service, the AI platform, the main brand page - had been built at different times, by different people, under different constraints. When we looked at them honestly, they weren't in great shape.

Security headers missing. Contrast ratios failing. ARIA attributes wrong. Meta tags incomplete. Performance held back by external fonts and unoptimized images. None of it was catastrophic on its own - but together, it meant Google was ranking the sites lower than they deserved, some browsers were flashing security warnings before visitors even landed, and screen reader users couldn't navigate key sections at all.

So we fixed them. All of them. And every fix got written down.

How the standard was built

Every fix became a rule.
Every rule became the build.

After enough audits, the patterns stopped being surprising. Security headers were missing on every site that didn't specifically set them. Contrast ratios failed on text that looked fine to the designer but failed WCAG AA at actual measurements. ARIA attributes were added by developers who had the right idea but the wrong implementation. Meta descriptions got skipped under deadline pressure.

So we stopped treating these as individual bugs and started treating them as a checklist - a build standard applied before the first line of HTML, not patched in after the audit. Self-hosted fonts. Explicit image dimensions. Security headers on every path. Muted text at minimum #8C8C8C, never #777777. Aria-labels on every interactive element without visible text.

The standard is now what we build from, not what we fix toward.

Plain terms

What these scores
actually do to your business.

Lighthouse scores aren't vanity metrics. Each one maps directly to something that costs you customers, ranking, or trust - usually all three.

Performance

Your site is too slow. People are leaving.

Every additional second of load time costs roughly 7% of visitors. Google uses page speed as a direct ranking factor - a slow site ranks lower than a fast one with identical content.

A 60% performance score isn't a yellow flag. It means your homepage takes 4-6 seconds to load on a mobile connection. Most people are gone by second 3.

SEO

Google can't read you. You don't exist.

SEO isn't just keywords. It's whether search engines can crawl your pages, understand what they're about, and trust that the content is legitimate. Missing meta tags, broken canonical URLs, and blocked robots.txt all count against you.

A 65% SEO score means Google is guessing at what half your pages are about. It's ranking you for things you didn't intend - or not ranking you at all.

Security

Open doors. Some browsers warn visitors before they arrive.

Missing security headers - HSTS, Content Security Policy, X-Frame-Options - leave your site vulnerable to clickjacking, script injection, and cross-origin attacks. Some of these gaps trigger browser-level warnings that visitors see before your page loads.

A 0% security score, like Colin Auto had at initial audit, means no protective headers at all. Any halfway-decent scanner flags your site as unsafe. That includes the scanners potential clients and partners run.

Accessibility

Part of your audience literally can't use your site.

Contrast failures, missing labels, broken keyboard navigation - these aren't edge cases. 15-20% of people have some form of disability that affects how they browse. Screen readers, keyboard navigation, and zoom usage are more common than most developers account for.

Beyond the human cost, ADA compliance is increasingly enforced in the US. Businesses have been successfully sued over inaccessible websites. A 70% accessibility score is a documented liability.

Best Practices

It works today. It breaks quietly over time.

Best Practices catches deprecated APIs, insecure HTTP requests, outdated libraries, and techniques browsers are actively phasing out. None of these cause immediate visible failures - they accumulate until something stops working and nobody knows why.

The site your developer handed over in 2019 has been aging against browser updates ever since. Best Practices is the score that tells you how much it's drifted.
